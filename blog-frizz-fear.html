Frizz-Fear Marketing

by Shambhavi Singh

Ever notice how every "frizz-free" ad looks like a keratin commercial?

Dissecting the Ad

The 72-hour promise.

TRESemmé promises '72 hrs Frizz Control' — but note the fine print: "lab tests vs non-conditioning shampoo, not vs salon-flat hair."

What the test actually measured
TRESemmé duo vs. a shampoo with zero conditioner
Not vs salon-straightened hair. Not vs the model's untreated hair. Just vs soap with no conditioner.
Any moisturising product would likely beat that control.

The 72-hour claim is based only on a lab comparison between the brand's own duo and a control shampoo that contains no conditioner at all — not a comparison to salon-straightened hair, which implies that "Frizz control up to 72 hrs" isn't a promise of pin-straight hair in real life.

It's just saying the brand's shampoo + conditioner performed better than a plain, non-conditioning shampoo under lab conditions. The sleek, mirror-smooth hair shown on-screen almost certainly received a professional blow-dry or keratin treatment. That look is not what the lab test measured.

By juxtaposing the 72-hour claim with a salon-level visual, the ad nudges viewers to assume "this bottle = that result" — even though the evidence only proves "this bottle is a bit less frizzy than plain soap."

Bottom line

The disclaimer reveals the claim is a controlled-lab comparison against a weak baseline — not proof you'll get keratin-treatment hair from a single wash at home. Moreover, the control shampoo contains no conditioning agents at all, so of course the TRESemmé duo looks superior because it has silicones and softeners that immediately weigh the cuticle down and disguise flyaways.

Now that we know this

Let's talk about frizz in general.

"I brushed my hair every school morning because that was 'neat' — thanks, Euro-centric beauty norms."

— Shambhavi Singh
The personal part

By lunch, I had a static halo — so I flat-ironed, fried my ends, and even did a DIY smoothing treatment at fourteen. Mom ordered the chemicals, and the whole family joined in.

Some years later, I was in college and I broke up with my straight-hair dreams sometime between my freshman and sophomore year when I saw other women with curly hair that looked so good, I was in awe. But really, it was a forced breakup because College = no time (or cash) for constant keratin touch-ups.

I finally whispered the truth: "Girl, you have curly hair." I ditched the brush and dove into a messy, TikTok-powered quest: low-poo cleansers, plopping fails, protein-moisture charts. Slowly, the halo softened into waves that belonged on my head.

I learned that curls need moisture + protein, not punishment. I discovered diffusing on low, microfibre towels, and ingredient lists I can pronounce. The result: the so-called "frizz" was just my curls begging for proper care. They were with me all this time — they just needed love and attention.

What I've learnt

After being on both poles of hair care — one being suffering your hair health for temporary looks, and the other being long-term care, even if it looks terrible initially — no shampoo bottle can duplicate salon keratin, because it's the flat iron + chemicals doing the work.

I'm done buying the shame narrative. Done buying generalised shampoo and conditioners. They aren't thinking about me, and I won't think about them either.

Here's to retiring the
straight-hair fantasy.
