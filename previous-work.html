— The brief
Context

Herberger Institute Student Success needed a series of infographics to help students navigate complex wellbeing topics — perfectionism, burnout, and imposter syndrome — in a way that felt accessible, visually engaging, and grounded in research.

The challenge was translating dense psychological concepts into designs that students would actually stop and read. Not simplified to the point of losing meaning, but not so clinical that they wouldn't connect.

11 pieces across three topic series. Each required a distinct visual approach — different enough to feel fresh, consistent enough to feel like a system.

"The goal was never to dumb it down. It was to make it land."

The series was also a learning arc. Over 11 infographics, I tracked my own design time against the quality of each output — and the pattern that emerged became part of the story: every piece got faster and better at the same time.

01 Performance over 11 pieces
What the data showed

As the series progressed, design time decreased and quality increased — consistently, across all 11 pieces.

The first infographic took approximately 6 hours. By piece 11, it took under 1 hour. Quality, as assessed by the supervisor, moved in the opposite direction — improving with each piece as I refined my understanding of the brief, the audience, and the visual system.

This is what deliberate practice looks like when it's documented. Not just getting faster — getting better faster.

— Full Plates — Brand, Website & Policy
The origin

Full Plates began as a Hackathon project in December 2024 — a team concept for connecting restaurants with surplus food to shelters, NGOs, and communities in need. The tagline: Feeding Communities, Fighting Waste.

After the hackathon, I took it in my own direction. I built out the full brand system and website prototype — 14 pages covering the mission, app design, zero-waste model, impact metrics, and a global growth roadmap — then extended it into a zine and policy brief exploring food redistribution through law, logistics, and design.

— Rattle (Robot+Cattle) — Speculative Worldbuilding
The project

In my junior year at ASU, I developed a world built around an imagined community food system — integrating social networks, wellness rituals, and emerging farm technologies. Across three documents, I mapped the world's geography and daily life, then dove into Rattle: an innovative natural-cultivation technology designed for Arizona's climate.

This project blends speculative narrative, systems design, and visual prototyping to explore how people might live, eat, and care for one another in a future food-centered culture. It sits at the intersection of design fiction, policy thinking, and technology concept work.

"What if a local farmer could control crop health from outside the crop area using Virtual Reality?"
Three documents, one world
01
Rattle (Robot+Cattle)
An Arduino-based autonomous farm robot that moves to infected plants, identifies disease via IoT sensors and ML algorithms, and sprays the appropriate pesticide — tending fields without manual labour.
02
Naturally Occurring Nurtured Foods
How can organic foods be made more affordable by changing the supply chain? Explores community farming models, vegetation incubation, and democratizing cultivation technology for Arizona's arid climate.
03
Naturopathy
A "third space" — shared green rooms and simple rituals where people swap cubicles for compost circles. Care farming as community infrastructure.

— De Novo Bakes — First Freelance Project
The brief

De Novo Bakes was my very first freelancing project — a cloud kitchen bakery run by Aakriti that needed a full menu card to match the warmth and personality of everything she baked. The brief: make it feel handcrafted, appetising, and easy to read at a glance.

The result was a 3-page menu book — cover, cakes, and everything else — with a soft yellow palette drawn directly from the brand, custom typography pairings, and clear dietary indicators for veg, egg, and non-veg items across 60+ products.

"My first client. My first brief. The one that made me realise design could be useful to real people building real things."
